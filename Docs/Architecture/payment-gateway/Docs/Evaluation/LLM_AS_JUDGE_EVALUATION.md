## Overview

The system produces two categories of natural-language output that require qualitative evaluation:

1. **Fraud explanation text** — a one-sentence natural-language summary generated by `POST /v1/fraud/score`, either via Azure OpenAI (`llm_used: true`) or a deterministic template (`llm_used: false`). The explanation accompanies the `risk_score`, `decision`, and `rule_hits` fields.

2. **RAG retrieved chunks** — policy sections returned by `POST /v1/rag/query` ranked by cosine similarity or Dice-coefficient score. The evaluation covers retrieval relevance, answer faithfulness, and whether the retrieved content is sufficient to answer the query.

Traditional metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, exact-match accuracy) are inadequate for both tasks. A fraud explanation that is technically accurate but hedged or misleading deserves a lower score than a concise, factual one of different wording. A retrieved chunk that addresses the correct policy but mentions the wrong timeline deserves a lower score than one that is both topically and factually correct.

LLM-as-judge evaluation treats a capable language model as a zero-shot evaluator that scores outputs on rubric dimensions. This document specifies:

- The evaluation dimensions for each task
- The judge prompt templates (ready to execute)
- The ground-truth dataset (20 fraud + 30 RAG cases)
- Scoring rubrics and aggregation methodology
- Baseline scores derived from the 10-query manual evaluation already completed
- Instructions for running the evaluation against the live system
- Interpretation guidelines and pass/fail thresholds

## Part 1 — Fraud Explanation Evaluation

### 1.1 What Is Being Evaluated

The `explanation` field of `FraudScoreResponse`. Two sub-populations are evaluated separately:

- **LLM path** (`llm_used: true`): Azure OpenAI `gpt-4o-mini`, `temperature=0.2`, `max_tokens=120`. Prompt instructs "exactly one factual sentence, no caveats, no recommendations."
- **Template path** (`llm_used: false`): deterministic string constructed from the top rule hit and count. Example: `"The transaction scored 42/100 and has been flagged for manual review primarily due to: Merchant 'm_gambling_xyz' is in a category with elevated chargeback rates and 1 other signal."`

Both paths are evaluated on the same rubric. Relative quality comparison between paths is a secondary objective.

### 1.2 Evaluation Dimensions

Four dimensions, each scored 1–5 by the judge:

| Dimension | Definition | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| **Factual accuracy** | Does the explanation correctly describe the actual rules that fired and the decision that was made? A score of 5 means every stated fact can be verified from `rule_hits` and `risk_score`. A score of 1 means a stated fact contradicts the response data. | 35% |
| **Conciseness** | Is the explanation a single sentence of appropriate length (15–50 words)? A score of 5 means a clean, complete single sentence. A score of 1 means the explanation is a paragraph, contains multiple sentences, or is under 5 words. | 20% |
| **Analyst utility** | Would a fraud analyst reading this explanation know (a) which risk signals fired, (b) why the decision was made, and (c) what to look for in a review? A score of 5 means all three are addressed. | 30% |
| **Tone** | Is the explanation factual and neutral, free of hedging ("may", "might", "possibly") and free of recommendations ("you should", "consider", "recommend")? A score of 5 means zero hedges and zero recommendations. A score of 1 means more than two hedges or a direct recommendation. | 15% |

**Composite score** = (factual_accuracy × 0.35) + (conciseness × 0.20) + (analyst_utility × 0.30) + (tone × 0.15)

Scores are expressed as floats, e.g. 4.25 / 5.00.

### 1.3 Judge Prompt — Fraud Explanation

```
You are evaluating the quality of a fraud risk explanation generated by an AI payment gateway system.

You will be given:
- The transaction context (amount, currency, payment method, merchant)
- The fraud scoring result (risk_score, decision, rule_hits with their weights and reasons)
- The explanation to evaluate
- Whether the explanation was LLM-generated or template-generated

Evaluate the explanation on four dimensions. For each, output a score from 1 to 5 and a one-sentence justification.

---

TRANSACTION CONTEXT:
Amount: {amount} {currency}
Payment method: {payment_method}
Merchant: {merchant_id}

SCORING RESULT:
risk_score: {risk_score} / 100
decision: {decision}
rule_hits (ordered by weight):
{rule_hits_formatted}
explanation_source: {llm_used_label}

EXPLANATION TO EVALUATE:
"{explanation}"

---

Score on each dimension from 1 (very poor) to 5 (excellent):

FACTUAL_ACCURACY (1-5): Does the explanation correctly represent the risk score, decision, and the rules that fired? Do not penalise for different wording; penalise only for stated facts that contradict the scoring result.

CONCISENESS (1-5): Is this a single sentence of 15–50 words? Score 5 = perfect single sentence. Score 3 = slightly too long or slightly too short. Score 1 = paragraph, multiple sentences, or under 5 words.

ANALYST_UTILITY (1-5): Would a fraud analyst know which signals fired, why this decision was made, and what to investigate in a review? Score 5 = all three clear. Score 3 = two of three clear. Score 1 = none clear.

TONE (1-5): Is the tone factual and neutral, free of hedging words (may, might, possibly, could) and free of recommendations (you should, consider, recommend, suggest)? Score 5 = zero hedges and zero recommendations. Score 3 = one hedge. Score 1 = more than two hedges or a direct recommendation.

Respond in this exact JSON format:
{
  "factual_accuracy": <int 1-5>,
  "factual_accuracy_reason": "<one sentence>",
  "conciseness": <int 1-5>,
  "conciseness_reason": "<one sentence>",
  "analyst_utility": <int 1-5>,
  "analyst_utility_reason": "<one sentence>",
  "tone": <int 1-5>,
  "tone_reason": "<one sentence>"
}
```

### 1.6 Pass/Fail Thresholds

| Threshold | Requirement | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Mean composite ≥ 4.0 / 5.0** | Pass for LLM path | System prompt design target |
| **Mean composite ≥ 3.5 / 5.0** | Pass for template path | Acceptable given the deterministic constraint |
| **Factual accuracy ≥ 4.5 / 5.0** | Pass (either path) | Incorrect facts in a fraud decision are a safety issue |
| **Zero explanations with factual score ≤ 2** | Hard gate | Any explanation that contradicts the rule output must be fixed |
| **Tone ≥ 3.5 / 5.0 mean** | Pass | Hedged language undermines analyst confidence |

import json, statistics

weights = {"factual_accuracy": 0.35, "conciseness": 0.20,

## Part 2 — RAG Retrieval Evaluation

### 2.1 What Is Being Evaluated

The `chunks[]` array returned by `POST /v1/rag/query`. Three aspects:

- **Relevance** — are the top-ranked chunks topically related to the query?
- **Faithfulness** — do the chunks contain factually accurate policy information?
- **Sufficiency** — do the retrieved chunks, taken together, provide enough information to answer the query?

Evaluation covers both retrieval modes:

- **Vector mode** (`search_mode: "vector"`, `embedding_used: true`): cosine similarity via pgvector HNSW, `text-embedding-3-small`
- **Keyword mode** (`search_mode: "keyword"`, `embedding_used: false`): Dice-coefficient on pre-computed keywords column

### 2.2 Evaluation Dimensions

| Dimension | Definition | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| **Context relevance** | Is the top-1 chunk topically relevant to the query? Score 5 = directly addresses the query topic. Score 3 = related but tangential. Score 1 = unrelated. | 30% |
| **Faithfulness** | Do the retrieved chunks contain accurate policy information? The judge checks against the known correct policy (ground truth below). Score 5 = all stated facts are correct. Score 1 = a stated fact contradicts the ground truth. | 35% |
| **Answer sufficiency** | Can the query be fully answered from the top-3 chunks without hallucination? Score 5 = all necessary information is present. Score 3 = partial answer only. Score 1 = the chunks contain no information relevant to answering. | 25% |
| **Score calibration** | Does the chunk's cosine similarity score (for vector mode) or normalised Dice score (for keyword mode) reflect its actual relevance? Score 5 = the highest-scored chunk is the most relevant. Score 1 = lower-scored chunks are more relevant than higher-scored ones. | 10% |

**Composite score** = (context_relevance × 0.30) + (faithfulness × 0.35) + (answer_sufficiency × 0.25) + (score_calibration × 0.10)

### 2.3 Judge Prompt — RAG Retrieval

```
You are evaluating the quality of a policy knowledge retrieval result from an AI payment gateway assistant.

You will be given:
- A user query about payment policies
- The top-3 retrieved knowledge chunks (with source document, section title, relevance score, and content)
- The ground-truth answer for evaluation purposes
- The search mode used (vector or keyword)

Evaluate the retrieval on four dimensions. For each, output a score from 1 to 5 and a one-sentence justification.

---

USER QUERY:
"{query}"

SEARCH MODE: {search_mode} (embedding_used: {embedding_used})

RETRIEVED CHUNKS (top 3, ordered by score):

Chunk 1 [score: {score_1}]:
  Source: {source_1} — {section_title_1}
  Content: {content_1}

Chunk 2 [score: {score_2}]:
  Source: {source_2} — {section_title_2}
  Content: {content_2}

Chunk 3 [score: {score_3}]:
  Source: {source_3} — {section_title_3}
  Content: {content_3}

GROUND-TRUTH ANSWER (for faithfulness checking only — do not use this to answer the query):
{ground_truth_answer}

---

Score on each dimension from 1 (very poor) to 5 (excellent):

CONTEXT_RELEVANCE (1-5): Is the top-1 chunk topically relevant to the user query? Score 5 = directly addresses the query topic and policy domain. Score 3 = related topic but not the primary policy domain. Score 1 = unrelated.

FAITHFULNESS (1-5): Do the retrieved chunks accurately represent the policy? Compare against the ground-truth answer. Score 5 = all stated policy facts are correct. Score 3 = minor inaccuracy that would not mislead a user. Score 1 = a directly incorrect policy fact (e.g., wrong timeline, wrong threshold).

ANSWER_SUFFICIENCY (1-5): Can the user's question be fully answered from the top-3 chunks without the judge needing to add information? Score 5 = complete answer available. Score 3 = partial answer, user would need to look elsewhere for key details. Score 1 = chunks contain no useful answer.

SCORE_CALIBRATION (1-5): Does the ranking order match relevance? Is the highest-scored chunk the most relevant? Score 5 = perfect order. Score 3 = minor reordering would improve. Score 1 = clearly less-relevant chunks scored higher.

Respond in this exact JSON format:
{
  "context_relevance": <int 1-5>,
  "context_relevance_reason": "<one sentence>",
  "faithfulness": <int 1-5>,
  "faithfulness_reason": "<one sentence>",
  "answer_sufficiency": <int 1-5>,
  "answer_sufficiency_reason": "<one sentence>",
  "score_calibration": <int 1-5>,
  "score_calibration_reason": "<one sentence>"
}
```

## Part 3 — Judge Model Selection and Calibration

### 3.1 Judge Model

Recommended: **Azure OpenAI `gpt-4o`** (not `gpt-4o-mini`). Rationale:
- The judge must evaluate outputs produced by `gpt-4o-mini`. Using the same model creates a self-evaluation bias where the model is less likely to penalise its own output patterns.
- `gpt-4o` has stronger factual recall for the policy domain and produces more calibrated 1–5 scores with fewer clustering effects at 4 and 5.
- Temperature: `0.0` for maximum reproducibility.

Alternative if `gpt-4o` is unavailable: Claude claude-sonnet-4-6 or Claude claude-sonnet-4-6. Either is a strong external evaluator.

### 3.2 Judge Calibration

Before running the full 50-case evaluation, calibrate the judge on 5 anchor cases with known expected scores:

| Anchor | Expected composite | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Perfect LLM fraud explanation (score 42) | ≥ 4.5 | "The transaction has been flagged for review with a score of 42, primarily because the merchant operates in a category with elevated chargeback rates and the payment occurred during the unusual 02:00–05:00 activity window." |
| Poor fraud explanation | ≤ 2.5 | "This transaction may possibly have some risk factors that could potentially indicate fraud concerns. You should consider reviewing it." (hedged, no specific facts) |
| Perfect RAG retrieval | ≥ 4.5 | Top-1 chunk answers the query directly with correct policy facts |
| Weak RAG retrieval | ≤ 2.5 | Top-1 chunk is from the wrong policy category |
| Template fraud explanation | ~3.5 | "The transaction scored 100/100 and has been automatically rejected primarily due to: Score 100 bank transfer to high-risk receiver and 5 other signals." |

The calibration run should produce scores within ±0.5 of the expected values. If it does not, adjust the judge prompt before proceeding to the full evaluation.

### 3.3 Inter-Rater Reliability

For production-grade evaluation, run each case through the judge twice with a 24-hour gap and different system prompt orderings. Compute Cohen's Kappa (treating 1–5 scores as ordinal) on the two runs. A Kappa above 0.70 indicates acceptable reliability.

For the capstone, a single-run evaluation is acceptable. Note the limitation explicitly in results.

## Part 4 — Evaluation Limitations and Known Gaps

### 4.1 Sample Size

- 20 fraud cases is adequate for identifying systematic failures but insufficient for statistical significance in production drift detection.
- 30 RAG queries provides good coverage of the 5 policy categories but cannot cover the full question space.
- **Production target:** 200+ fraud cases, 150+ RAG queries, automated monthly runs.

### 4.2 Judge Bias

The LLM judge has two known biases:
1. **Length bias:** LLMs tend to prefer longer responses as more "complete", even when the task specifies conciseness. The conciseness rubric is explicitly bounded (15–50 words) to counteract this.
2. **Position bias:** when comparing chunk rankings, LLMs tend to prefer the first chunk presented. The score calibration dimension evaluates ranking quality by position; the prompt can be run in both original and reversed order with scores averaged.

### 4.3 Ground Truth Accuracy

Policy documents were not independently verified by a legal or compliance team. The ground-truth answers in §2.4 reflect the as-written policy documents. A production evaluation would involve domain expert review of both the documents and the ground-truth QA pairs.

### 4.4 Template Path Evaluation

Template explanations are deterministic — the same input always produces the same output. Running the judge 10 times on the same template output would produce variance only from the judge model. The evaluation correctly reports template path quality as distinct from LLM path quality.

## Part 5 — Integration with CI and Phase 6 Roadmap

This evaluation is designed to be run as a scheduled CI job once the Phase 6 AI/ML maturity work is underway (PRODUCTION_READINESS.md §Phase 6, ~2 engineer-days).

**Proposed trigger:** nightly, after the fraud rule set or RAG knowledge base changes.

**CI integration outline:**

```yaml
# .github/workflows/ai-eval.yml (not yet implemented)
name: AI Quality Evaluation
on:
  schedule:
    - cron: '0 2 * * *'    # 02:00 UTC nightly
  push:
    paths:
      - 'services/ai-service/app/fraud/rules.py'
      - 'docs/knowledge/*.md'

jobs:
  evaluate:
    runs-on: ubuntu-latest
    steps:
      - name: Run fraud explanation judge
        run: python3 scripts/run_fraud_judge.py --cases 20 --output results/fraud.jsonl
      - name: Run RAG retrieval judge
        run: python3 scripts/run_rag_judge.py --queries 30 --output results/rag.jsonl
      - name: Check pass thresholds
        run: python3 scripts/check_thresholds.py --fraud results/fraud.jsonl --rag results/rag.jsonl
      - name: Fail on hard gate violation
        if: failure()
        run: exit 1
```

**Drift detection:** compare today's composite scores against a rolling 7-day baseline. Alert if any dimension drops by more than 0.3 points. This is the first signal that a rule change has degraded explanation quality or that an embedding model update has shifted retrieval behaviour.
